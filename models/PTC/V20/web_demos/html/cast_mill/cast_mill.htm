Milling of Casting and Forgings


Once a casting or forging returns from the foundry, a manufacturer will typically need to machine details such as flats and holes that could not be included in the cast or forged design. This type of machining usually does not involve surfacing or extensive cavity milling, and most 4 and 5 axis work is positioning, not contouring. Another consideration is a possible variation of stock size. Instead of starting from some exact stock piece that was milled flat, a casting or forging can have a fairly large variation. This could mean that a facing operation might need to start some amount above the finish surface to account for extra material.



Rough and Finished Casting
In this example, a large industrial valve is mounted vertically on a rotary table, and  a horizontal machining center will be used to finish machine the part. First let's notice that we have both the cast version and the finished version. These two parts will be assembled in manufacturing superimposed on top of each other.
 
 


Precise Facing Toolpaths
Facing Flats
This functionality allows us to program looking at either version. For this example, the cast version was created using the Family Table functionality. First, we will use a 2 inch shell mill to face of the various flats, starting with the vertical boss at B0. We will sketch a path right on the part surface, requesting a plunge move into the material, cutting down, and then retracting. Working in 3 dimensions, the system automatically knows we are programming  at the B0 index position.
 

Next, we move to the 2 square shaped flats at B45. Using the same process, we sketch the desired paths, in this case two of them, walking onto and then around the first flat, pulling out 2 inches to clear the bulge in the middle of the part, and then re-engaging the part to mill the bottom flat. We have a full range of customization tools that will allow for any sort of incremental or absolute moves, retracts and plunges, and others.
 

Moving on to B90, let's assume that more than one pass is necessary to face this side. This could be due to either designed excess or maybe we're simply getting substantial variation among the castings, and we need to program in a couple of rough passes before the finish pass. First, we define our motion as before.
 

Now I will modify my machining parameters to specify a step depth of 0.050" and 4 passes. As we replay the path, you can see that now I have multiple passes.
 
 

Workcell Management
From there, we go around the part, milling off all of the flats. We can view the toolpaths individually as we have seen, or we can view them all as one operation.
 
 

Center Drilling
Now it's time to start drilling. I'll start with a center drill and work at B270, where we left off with the milling. My drill start height is set by picking on the milled flat, and for center drill work I specify a  countersink diameter.
 

Drill and Tap
Staying in this orientation, next I'll pick a pilot drill and then a tap for the large bolt circle. Pro/MFG understands the full suite of drilling and tapping cycles, including peck drilling, tapping, boring, countersinking, and others. Now I'll play all the drilling cycles at B270.
 

Toolpath Optimization

Continuing on, I'll create the drilling sequences on the rest of the part. Notice what we have done: at first, we programmed with one tool all the way around the part. But now I have created drill sequences based on table rotation. At B270, we center drilled, drilled, and tapped, then we repeated that process at B0. This was the easy way to do the programming, but we want the program to do complete all operations at all rotation angles with each tool, minimizing tool changes. With Pro/MFG, this is also simply a programmer selection. As you will see, I will tell the system to minimize tool changes and what order to put them in. When we play the entire operation, you will see that it does just that.
